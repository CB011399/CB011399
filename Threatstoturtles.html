Threats to Turtles

Seven different species of sea (or marine) turtles grace our ocean waters, from the shallow seagrass beds of the Indian Ocean, to the colorful reefs of the Coral Triangle and the sandy beaches of the Eastern Pacific. While these highly migratory species periodically come ashore to either bask or nest, sea turtles spend the bulk of their lives in the ocean. Over the last 200 years, human activities have tipped the scales against the survival of these ancient mariners. Slaughtered for their eggs, meat, skin, and shells, sea turtles suffer from poaching and over-exploitation.


They also face habitat destruction and accidental capture—known as bycatch—in fishing gear. Climate change has an impact on turtle nesting sites; it alters sand temperatures, which then affects the sex of hatchlings. Nearly all species of sea turtle are now classified as endangered, with three of theseven existing species being critically endangered. Sea turtles continue to be harvested unsustainably both for human consumption and trade of their parts. Turtle meat and eggs are a source of food and income for many people around the world. Some also kill turtles for medicine and religious ceremonies. Tens of thousands of sea turtles are lost this way every year, devastating populations of already endangered greens and hawksbills. Killing of turtles for both domestic and international markets continues as well. International trade in all sea turtle species and their parts is prohibited under the Convention on International Trade in Endangered Species of Wild Fauna and Flora (CITES), a global agreement among governments to regulate or ban international trade in species under threat. Still, illegal trafficking persists.

Bycatch

Fishermen sometimes catch and discard animals they do not want, cannot sell, or are not allowed to keep. This is collectively known as “bycatch.” Bycatch can be fish, but also includes other animals such as dolphins, whales, sea turtles, and seabirds that become hooked or entangled in fishing gear. Bycatch can negatively affect species such as dolphins, sea turtles, protected fish, and whales by harming animals, contributing to population declines, and impeding population recovery. Other impacts of fisheries on marine mammals may include removal of their preferred prey and sometimes habitat damage.


Bycatch refers to “discarded catch of marine species and unobserved mortality due to a direct encounter with fishing vessels and gear.” These unintentionally caught animals often suffer injuries or die. Unwanted catch is an issue both ecologically and economically. Animals that are discarded often die and cannot reproduce, impacting marine ecosystems. Bycatch can slow the rebuilding of overfished stocks, and place protected species such as whales and sea turtles at further risk. Bycatch of species like corals and sponges can cause damage to protected corals and to important fish habitat.

Conservation and Prevention

Developing and implementing turtle-friendly gear such as using Turtle Excluder Devices(TEDs) in trawl nets or circle hooks in longline fisheries,can reduce turtle bycatch.



Governments and fisheries management organizations can establish regulations and standards to minimize bycatch.Strict enforcement of these regulations is essential to ensure compliance.



Rising awareness among fishermen about the importance of sea turtle conservation and providing them with training on bycatch mitigation techniques can help reduce unintentional captures.



Continued research and development of innovative fishing technologies can lead to more effective and selective gear that minimizes bycatch without compromising catch effectively.



Cooprention among countries and international organizations is crucial for addressing the issue of sea turtle bycatch,as sea turtles migrate across multiple jurisdictions.Sharing best practices and knowledge can facilitate the adoption of effective conservation measures.

Vessel Strikes

Vessel strikes are a significant threat to turtle populations around the world.These accidents occur when boats and other watercraft collide with turtles,causing serious injuries or death.Turtles are particularly vulnerable to vessel strikes,because they are slow moving and often surface to breathe,making them difficult to spot from distance.


Vessel strikes can have devastating consequences for turtle populations which are already under threat from habitat loss,pollution and other human activities.When turtles are killed or injured by boats it can distrupt the natural balance of marine ecosystems and reduce the overall healthof these fragile environments.To protect the turtle populations and their habitats,it is essential to take steps to prevent vessel strikes and support conservation efforts that promote responsibility boating practices and habitat protection.

Conservation and Prevention

To prevent vessel strikes,it's important to operate watercraft at safe speeds,be vigilant for wildlife and avoid areas where turtles are known to frequent.



Conservation efforts can also help protect turtle populations and their habitats.



Supporting organizations that work to protect turtle populations and their habitats is an important part of conservation.



Participating in beach clean-ups can help remove debris that can harm turtles and other wildlife.



Educating others about the importance of protecting turtle populations and their habitats can also help to prevent vessel strikes.

solution

Light and Noise Pollution

Light and noise pollution are significant threats to sea turtles,which are highly sensitive to changes in their environment.Light pollution can disorient hatchlings as they emerge from their nests,causing them to become confused and headin the wrong direction.This can make it difficult for them to reach the ocean and can increase their risk of prediction.Light pollution can also disrupt the nesting behavior of adult turtles,causing them to abandon their nests or lay their eggs in unsuitable locations.


Noise pollution can also have a significant impact on sea turtles which rely on sound to communicate,navigate and locate prey.KLoud noises from boats,construction sites and other human activities can disrupt these vital processes and cause stress and disorientation.This can make it difficult for turtles to find food,mate and navigate to their nesting sites.To protect sea turtles from the effects of light and noise pollution,it is essential to take steps to reduce these impacts and create more turtle-friendly environments.

Conservation and Prevention

To prevent light pollution,use turtle-friendly lighting that is low in intensity,long-wavelength and shielded to minimize glare.



To prevent noise pollution,avoid operating boats and other watercraft near turtle habitats and nesting sites.



Support conservation efforts that work to protect turtle populations and their habitats from the impacts of light and noise pollution.



Participate in beach clean-ups to help remove debris taht can harm turtles and other wildlife.



Educate others about the importance of protecting turtle populations and their habitats from the effects of light and noise pollution.
